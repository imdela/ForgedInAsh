# Chapter Five: First Demonstration

She had taken a different route this morning.

Not because she had reason to vary it — the mark was behaving at its unexplained baseline warm, which was becoming so consistent she was almost categorizing it as normal, which was a thing she was choosing not to do yet — but because she had woken up three minutes before her usual time and had found herself doing the walk-planning she usually did the night before. She had changed the route, and then she had changed it back, and then she had left at the same time she always left, through the same passages, and arrived four minutes early the same way she had arrived four minutes early yesterday.

This was the same as yesterday, which meant it was already a pattern, which meant he would have already factored it in. She noted that. She sat down in her chair — the one she had decided was hers on the first day, the one facing the door — and she put her hands flat on the table and she waited.

He arrived at the agreed hour. Not a minute before, not a minute after.

He sat down. He put the notebook on the table. He put the folding knife next to it, blade closed, and left it there. She had observed, over the previous two sessions, that the knife appeared before the notebook was open and disappeared when the notebook was closed, which meant it was associated with the waiting-and-observing portion of his work rather than the documenting portion. She had been building a catalog of his habits with the same methodical attention she gave to everything else, because habits were the most reliable data about a person. Habits were what the body did when the mind was elsewhere.

She had three items on the habit-catalog so far: the knife, the pencil appearing from the coat's interior left pocket, and the way he positioned the notebook at a slight angle from the table's edge — always the same angle, always the same position, before he opened it. She did not yet know what the angle told her. She noted it.

"Day two," he said.

"Day two," she agreed.

He opened the notebook and turned to a fresh page. The pencil appeared from the left pocket — she had confirmed the location today — and he held it and looked at her and waited.

She had thought about what to show him today.

The decision had taken most of the previous evening. The mark at Stage Two had a range of capabilities she had mapped over eighteen months of private testing: warm, burn, orient, direct, and two others she had not yet named because she did not have enough data about their parameters.

She had a hierarchy for what she was hiding. At the top: the two unnamed capabilities. She had only produced them three times each, in the eighteenth month of testing, and both times the production had been involuntary — triggered by something she had not been able to isolate. She did not know their parameters. She did not know their triggers. She was not going to demonstrate something she could not control. In the middle of the hierarchy: the upper range of the directional capability, which she had tested to its limit once and had stopped testing because the limit had felt like something she needed to be more prepared for before she touched it again. At the bottom of the hierarchy, not because it was the least significant but because she was the most uncertain about it: the baseline warmth. The secondary signal. The thing that oriented in his direction and had been doing it since Tuesday morning. That one was not hidden because it was dangerous. It was hidden because she did not understand it, and she was not going to hand him information she did not understand.

She was going to show him the directional capacity. Not because it was the most significant thing she could show — it wasn't, by a considerable margin — but because it was the most verifiable. He could measure heat at a specific point on a specific surface. The documentation would show the mark doing something the existing records apparently did not account for, which meant the data she was providing was genuinely new, which meant the deal was delivering what she had promised it would deliver.

This was how you held a deal together. You delivered early and you delivered something measurable. You made yourself more useful in the first three days than they had expected you to be in ten, so that by day four you were the person who had been exceeding the agreement rather than the person the agreement was barely covering. She had been running this logic in the information economy for five years. It applied here the same way it applied everywhere, with the additional variable that in this case exceeding the agreement had to stay within the forty-percent ceiling she was holding.

The directional capacity was approximately at the ceiling of what forty percent of her range could produce. Showing him something at the ceiling of forty percent established the ceiling convincingly while leaving the upper sixty percent completely invisible.

"I'm going to concentrate the heat at a specific point," she said. "Pick a surface."

He looked at the table. "Here." He put his finger on the edge, near the corner.

She focused on the mark and she focused on the corner of the table.

It took four seconds. The mark responded more readily now than it had in the first year of controlled testing. Fact, filed: the demonstrations are already accelerating the transition.

The corner of the table warmed. She could feel it through the wood — not through her hands, which were flat on the surface two feet away, but through the mark, which had its own quality of feedback that she had spent eighteen months learning to read. The wood didn't smoke. It just became warm at the point she had targeted and nowhere else.

Calder moved. He put two fingers on the corner of the table, then moved them one inch along the edge.

"Still warm here," he said.

"That's the edge of the cone. I can tighten it."

He took his fingers off the table. "Show me."

She tightened it. The radius shrank to approximately the size of a coin. The temperature at the point went up to compensate, which she had not intended and noted immediately: the mark maintained total heat output when the area decreased. She would need to account for that when she was deciding what not to show — because if the intensity-per-area relationship held at higher activation levels, the upper range of the directional capability was significantly more destructive than the lower range, and she needed to understand the relationship fully before she demonstrated anything in its vicinity.

"The point is hotter," he said.

"Yes."

He was writing. He had not put his finger back on the surface to verify. He had read the temperature from her face. She filed it under: he reads people accurately and uses it without announcing it.

She brought the mark back to baseline.

"Come closer," she said. "The next part is easier to observe from this side of the table."

He moved his chair at an angle, which put him approximately eighteen inches from her left shoulder. She had made the offer because she needed him to see the surface of the mark during activation, not because she had been prepared for the specific distance that resulted.

She activated the mark at low intensity.

The secondary signal arrived immediately.

Not the burn. Not the alarm. The orientation — the compass-quality she had been filing under unexplained for three days — steady and directed at a point approximately eighteen inches to her left. She kept her face level. She kept the activation steady. She noted the signal with the clinical detachment of someone who had been maintaining a list of unexplained observations and was adding to it. She noted also that the signal at this range was significantly stronger than it had been at range before. At eighteen inches, the signal was strong enough that she had to allocate active attention to not reacting to it.

Calder was watching the shoulder. His pencil was moving. She watched his pencil the way he watched the mark — with the focused attention of someone reading a document, not looking at a person. He wrote for approximately thirty seconds without looking up, then stopped and looked at the surface of the mark more carefully, from the angle his repositioned chair gave him.

He was very still when he looked at the mark directly. She noted this: during observation at this range, the small movements he normally maintained — the minor adjustments of posture, the pencil between his fingers — stopped. She had not seen him fully still before. The quality of his stillness was different from the operational stillness of the south passage. This was the stillness of someone who had noticed something and was in the process of deciding whether to name it.

She brought the activation slightly higher, enough to produce visible flush at the mark site. The secondary signal strengthened proportionally. She noted that: it scales with activation intensity.

He stopped writing. He was still watching the shoulder, but the pencil had stopped.

She noted the moment the pencil stopped with the precision of someone who had been waiting for it. She had known, from the pattern of the previous two sessions, that there was a threshold at which his documenting behavior changed. She had not known what the threshold was. Now she knew: it was somewhere in the range between first and second intensity, in close proximity. When the mark crossed that threshold, he stopped writing.

She noted that stopping writing was not the same as stopping observing. He was still watching the shoulder with the same focused attention. He had simply stopped putting the observation into words.

She brought the mark back down.

"That's the limit for today," she said.

He looked up. He looked at her for a moment — not at the shoulder, at her face — and then he looked back at the notebook and wrote something. Short entry. One line, possibly two. He put a mark at the end of it. She couldn't see what kind.

She had seen him put that same mark twice now. The same unfinished notation. It was near the top of her list of things she needed to know.

"The directionality," he said, "is not in any documentation I have."

"I know."

"How long have you been able to do it."

"That's relevant to the demonstrations."

He looked up. "Yes," he said.

"Two years. Approximately."

He wrote.

"The warm at a distance," she said. "The directionality. Both developed in the past two years without deliberate activation. They came in without me doing anything to produce them."

He wrote something longer. She noted: that piece of information was significant to him. She noted that she had not decided in advance to give it, and she noted that she had given it anyway, and she noted that she was going to have to examine why and whether it had been correct.

That examination was for later.

· ·

The session ended without a second demonstration.

She had shown him the directionality. She had shown him the scaling. She had given him the two-year timeline. That was sufficient for day two.

He closed the notebook. He picked up the folding knife from the table, opened it, and turned it once in his hand.

She had been watching for this. The knife had been on the table for the full duration of both demonstrations. He had not touched it. She had observed in the previous sessions that he touched it during waiting and observation. During the demonstrations, he had not touched it.

She was going to track it precisely across all remaining sessions.

"The documentation doesn't account for the development timeline," he said. He was not looking at her. He was looking at the notebook, closed in front of him. "It doesn't account for the directionality. It doesn't account for the scaling differential."

"I know," she said.

"The data you're providing is new."

"I know."

He was quiet for a moment. The knife turned once more. "Tomorrow," he said.

She stood up. She picked up her coat. She was almost at the door when he said: "You should bring water. The activation produces visible perspiration above the third intensity level."

She stopped.

She turned back.

He was still looking at the notebook. He said it the way he said most things — as a practical observation, entirely accurate, delivered without any apparent awareness of the fact that she had been managing visible perspiration from the mark for twenty years and the suggestion that she could address it by bringing water was the least useful piece of information anyone had ever offered her on the subject. It was correct. It was also the kind of advice you gave someone who had been dealing with this condition for three days rather than twenty years, which told her something about how he was modeling her situation that she had not known before he said it.

She should not laugh. There was no reason to laugh. The observation was correct. The delivery was impeccable. He had no idea why she was standing in the doorway not moving.

She managed to not laugh for approximately two seconds.

"Thank you," she said, and left.

She laughed in the passage outside.

It lasted about four steps and then she stopped because the district was not a place where you laughed alone in passages without a visible reason, and the laugh had surprised her more than it had come from her. She stood at the corner of the inner corridor and she noted the laugh the way she noted everything — fact, filed, implication pending — and then she noted that the implication was going directly to the later-list and she was not looking at it.

The later-list had grown today. The items, in approximate order of urgency: the secondary signal scaling with proximity and intensity, the unfinished notation at the end of his entries, the second section of the notebook, the knife pattern during demonstrations, the reason she had volunteered the two-year development timeline without deciding to, the thing she had seen in his stillness when he looked at the mark directly, and the laugh.

The laugh was at the bottom of the list because it was the least urgent and the most inconvenient. She was keeping it there.

The district looked the way it always looked at the mid-morning hour — the market in its second wave, the vendors settling into the steady middle of the day. She walked through it with the specific quality of attention that was not alertness and was not distraction but something between them: present, cataloguing, not looking for anything in particular and therefore available to notice anything that was there.

She was, she noted, doing the counting. She had not consciously decided to run the full count — every person in eyeline, every entrance and exit, everything that had changed from the day before. She was doing it because she had been doing it for five years and it had become automatic, and it was only now, walking home from the second session, that she noticed she was doing it and also noticed that she was not concerned about anything she was counting. She was counting out of habit. The mark was warm and calm at its unexplained baseline and she was counting the market because that was what she did, and neither of those things required active attention, which meant she had attention left over for the later-list.

She had eight days left. The mark was warm all the way home.

· ·

Eight days.

She changed the bandage. She sat at the small table. She ran the day.

Directional demonstration — new documentation produced. Goal met. He had written something and marked it with the unfinished notation she had now seen twice. She still did not know what kind of mark it was.

Secondary signal — scaled with activation intensity at close range. Strong enough at second intensity level to require active management of her expression. Cost to factor into remaining sessions. She noted also: his stillness during high-intensity observation was qualitatively different from his stillness during low-intensity observation. She noted it and added it to the list.

Knife pattern — confirmed across two demonstrations. He did not touch the knife during demonstrations.

She noted, separately, that he had stopped moving the pencil during the higher activation. She noted that the two things — the knife and the pencil — had stopped at the same intensity level.

She went to sleep.

The mark was still warm.

She stopped noting it for the night.
